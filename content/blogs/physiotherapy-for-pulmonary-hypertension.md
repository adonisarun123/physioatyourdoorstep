Pregnancy brings joy and anticipation, but for women diagnosed with pulmonary hypertension, it also introduces complex medical challenges that require specialized care and management. Understanding the role of physiotherapy in pulmonary hypertension during pregnancy has become increasingly crucial as more women with this condition seek to have healthy pregnancies and deliveries.

Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a serious cardiovascular condition characterized by elevated blood pressure in the pulmonary arteries, the vessels that carry blood from the heart to the lungs. When combined with pregnancy, this condition creates a unique set of challenges that demand careful monitoring, specialized medical care, and therapeutic interventions that can help manage symptoms while ensuring the safety of both mother and baby.

## Understanding Pulmonary Hypertension in Pregnancy

### What Is Pulmonary Hypertension?

Pulmonary hypertension occurs when the blood pressure in the pulmonary arteries becomes abnormally high, typically measuring above 25 mmHg at rest. This increased pressure forces the right side of the heart to work harder to pump blood through the lungs, potentially leading to right heart failure if left untreated.

The condition is classified into five distinct groups based on its underlying cause:

1. Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH) – Often genetic or associated with other conditions
2. PH due to left heart disease – Resulting from conditions affecting the left side of the heart
3. PH due to lung diseases – Associated with chronic lung conditions
4. Chronic thromboembolic PH (CTEPH) – Caused by blood clots in the lungs
5. PH with unclear mechanisms – Multiple or unknown causes

### The Pregnancy Challenge

Pregnancy naturally places additional stress on the cardiovascular system. Blood volume increases by approximately 40-50%, heart rate elevates, and cardiac output rises significantly. For women with pulmonary hypertension, these normal physiological changes can become life-threatening.

Recent studies indicate that maternal mortality rates in pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension range from 12-20%, with idiopathic pulmonary arterial hypertension carrying the highest risk. The condition is associated with:

- Major adverse cardiac events affecting up to 25% of pregnant women with PH
- Premature delivery occurs in approximately 58% of pregnancies
- Intrauterine fetal demise and low birth weight complications
- Heart failure during pregnancy in about 15% of cases
- Intensive care unit admission requirements for over 35% of patients

## The Critical Role of Physiotherapy in Management

### Evidence-Based Benefits

Research consistently demonstrates that exercise-based rehabilitation can provide significant benefits for individuals with pulmonary hypertension. A comprehensive review of clinical studies revealed that supervised physiotherapy programs led to:

- 48.5-meter improvement in six-minute walk distance
- 2.07 mL/kg/min increase in peak oxygen uptake
- 9.69 W enhancement in peak power output
- Improved quality of life scores on both physical and mental health assessments
- No increased risk of serious adverse events when properly supervised

### Specialized Physiotherapy Approaches During Pregnancy

Physiotherapy for pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension requires a highly individualized approach that considers both the cardiovascular limitations imposed by PH and the unique physiological changes of pregnancy. The therapeutic strategy must balance maintaining physical function with ensuring maternal and fetal safety.

#### Low-Impact Cardiovascular Training

Carefully monitored cardiovascular exercise forms the foundation of physiotherapy intervention. This typically includes:

- Supervised walking programs with heart rate and oxygen saturation monitoring
- Stationary cycling at controlled intensities
- Pool-based exercises where water buoyancy reduces cardiovascular stress
- Breathing exercises to optimize respiratory function

#### Strength and Flexibility Training

Maintaining muscle strength and flexibility becomes particularly important during pregnancy with PH:

- Light resistance training using bands or small weights
- Prenatal yoga modifications suitable for cardiovascular limitations
- Pelvic floor strengthening exercises to prepare for delivery
- Posture improvement techniques to accommodate pregnancy changes

#### Functional Movement Training

As pregnancy progresses, maintaining the ability to perform daily activities safely becomes crucial:

- Transfer techniques for getting in and out of bed safely
- Energy conservation strategies for household activities
- Safe movement patterns to prevent falls and injury
- Breathing coordination with physical activities

## Safety Considerations and Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications

Certain conditions make exercise inadvisable for pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension:

- Severe, uncontrolled pulmonary hypertension with right heart failure
- Recent hospitalization for cardiac complications
- Significant arrhythmias or unstable cardiac rhythm
- Severe hypoxemia requiring continuous oxygen therapy
- Preeclampsia or severe pregnancy-induced hypertension
- Placental complications such as placenta previa or abruption

### Relative Contraindications

Some conditions require careful evaluation and may allow modified exercise programs:

- Mild to moderate right heart dysfunction
- Controlled arrhythmias
- Previous episodes of heart failure now stabilized
- Mild hypoxemia is manageable with supplemental oxygen
- Pregnancy complications requiring close monitoring

### Warning Signs to Stop Exercise

Immediate cessation of physical activity is necessary if any of the following symptoms occur:

- Chest pain or significant discomfort
- Severe shortness of breath beyond expected levels
- Dizziness or feeling faint
- Heart palpitations or irregular heartbeat
- Severe fatigue or weakness
- Headache or visual disturbances
- Vaginal bleeding or uterine contractions
- Reduced fetal movement (in later pregnancy)

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Is exercise safe during pregnancy with pulmonary hypertension?

Exercise can be safe and beneficial for pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension when properly supervised by healthcare professionals. The key is individualized assessment and monitoring. Physiotherapy in pulmonary hypertension during pregnancy requires specialized expertise to balance the benefits of physical activity with the cardiovascular limitations imposed by PH. Always consult with your medical team before beginning any exercise program

### What are the warning signs that require immediate medical attention?

Pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension should seek immediate medical care if they experience severe shortness of breath, chest pain, fainting or dizziness, heart palpitations, severe swelling, or any signs of preterm labor. These symptoms could indicate worsening PH or pregnancy complications requiring urgent intervention

### Can physiotherapy help reduce the risk of complications during pregnancy with PH?

Yes, evidence-based physiotherapy can help improve cardiovascular fitness, maintain functional capacity, and potentially reduce the risk of complications. Studies show that supervised exercise programs can improve walking distance, oxygen uptake, and quality of life without increasing adverse events. However, the program must be carefully designed and monitored by experienced professionals

### How often should physiotherapy sessions occur during pregnancy with PH?

The frequency of physiotherapy sessions varies based on individual needs, disease severity, and pregnancy stage. Typically, sessions may occur 2-3 times per week initially, with frequency adjusted based on response and pregnancy progression. Regular monitoring and assessment help determine the optimal schedule for each patient.

### What types of exercises are recommended for pregnant women with pulmonary hypertension?

Recommended exercises typically include low-impact cardiovascular activities like walking or stationary cycling, breathing exercises, gentle strength training with light weights or resistance bands, and flexibility exercises. Pool-based activities can be particularly beneficial due to the supportive properties of water. All exercises should be performed under professional supervision.

### What should I expect during a physiotherapy assessment for pregnancy with PH?

A comprehensive assessment typically includes review of medical history, current symptoms, functional capacity testing (such as a modified six-minute walk test), heart rate and blood pressure responses to activity, and evaluation of pregnancy-related changes. The physiotherapist will also assess home environment and daily activity needs.

### How does physiotherapy coordinate with other medical treatments for PH during pregnancy?

Physiotherapy works as part of a multidisciplinary team approach. The physiotherapist coordinates closely with obstetricians, cardiologists, and PH specialists to ensure exercise programs complement medical treatments. Regular communication helps adjust programs based on medication changes, pregnancy progression, and overall health status.

### Are there any activities that should be completely avoided during pregnancy with PH?

Yes, certain activities should be avoided including high-intensity exercise, activities at high altitude, contact sports, exercises requiring lying flat on the back (especially after first trimester), activities with high fall risk, and any exercise that causes severe shortness of breath or chest pain.

### Can family members be involved in the physiotherapy program?

Absolutely. Family involvement is often beneficial and encouraged. Family members can learn proper techniques, understand warning signs, provide motivation and support, and assist with home exercise programs. This involvement helps create a supportive environment for successful pregnancy management.

### What happens to the physiotherapy program after delivery?

Postpartum physiotherapy focuses on gradual recovery and return to pre-pregnancy activity levels while managing ongoing PH. The program may address recovery from delivery, cardiovascular reconditioning, and adaptation to new motherhood challenges. Close monitoring continues as the body adjusts to post-pregnancy physiological changes.
